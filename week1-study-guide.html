Review: Factoring Polynomials

Factoring means rewriting a polynomial as a product of simpler polynomials. It's the reverse of multiplying. You should know three patterns cold.

The three patterns GCF (greatest common factor): 6x³ + 9x² = 3x²(2x + 3) Difference of squares: a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b) Trinomial (when leading coeff = 1): x² + bx + c = (x + p)(x + q) where p + q = b and p · q = c
Key idea

When you factor a trinomial x² + bx + c, you're asking: what two numbers multiply to c and add to b? That's the whole game. Everything else is variations on that question.

Worked Example — Trinomial
Factor: x² − x − 12
Need two numbers that multiply to −12, add to −1. Try: (−4) × (3) = −12 ✓ (−4) + (3) = −1 ✓ Answer: (x − 4)(x + 3) // Check by expanding: x² + 3x − 4x − 12 = x² − x − 12 ✓
Watch out

When the leading coefficient isn't 1 — like 2x² + 7x + 3 — you can't just look for two numbers. You need to factor by grouping or use the AC method. This trips students up because they try to apply the simple rule and it doesn't work.

AC Method — when leading coefficient ≠ 1 Factor: 2x² + 7x + 3 Step 1: Multiply a · c = 2 · 3 = 6 Step 2: Find two numbers that multiply to 6, add to 7: → 1 and 6 Step 3: Rewrite the middle term: 2x² + x + 6x + 3 Step 4: Factor by grouping: x(2x + 1) + 3(2x + 1) = (x + 3)(2x + 1)
Practice — Factoring
  1. x² + 5x + 6
  2. x² − 9
  3. 3x² − 12x
  4. 2x² − 5x − 3
  5. x² − 16 (trick: don't forget this is difference of squares)

Polynomial Long Division & Synthetic Division

Just as you can divide integers (17 ÷ 5 = 3 remainder 2), you can divide polynomials. The result has the same structure: a quotient and a remainder.

The general form: if you divide polynomial f(x) by d(x), you get

f(x) = d(x) · q(x) + r(x) where the degree of r(x) is less than the degree of d(x) (This is the polynomial division algorithm — same idea as integer division)
Worked Example — Long Division
Divide: (2x³ − 3x² + x − 5) ÷ (x − 2)
How many times does x go into 2x³? → 2x² Multiply: 2x²(x − 2) = 2x³ − 4x² Subtract: (2x³ − 3x²) − (2x³ − 4x²) = x² Bring down: x² + x How many times does x go into x²? → x Multiply: x(x − 2) = x² − 2x Subtract: (x² + x) − (x² − 2x) = 3x Bring down: 3x − 5 How many times does x go into 3x? → 3 Multiply: 3(x − 2) = 3x − 6 Subtract: (3x − 5) − (3x − 6) = 1 Result: 2x² + x + 3, remainder 1 // So: (2x³−3x²+x−5) = (x−2)(2x²+x+3) + 1
Synthetic Division — the shortcut

When the divisor is linear (x − c), you can use synthetic division: a compressed algorithm that works only with the coefficients. It's much faster once you get the hang of it, and it's what you'll use 90% of the time.

Synthetic Division — same problem: (2x³ − 3x² + x − 5) ÷ (x − 2) Divisor is (x − 2), so c = 2 Write coefficients of dividend: 2 | 2 -3 1 -5 | 4 2 6 +-------------------- 2 1 3 1 ↑remainder Read result: quotient = 2x² + x + 3, remainder = 1 ✓ Rule: bring down first coeff, then multiply by c and add down each column.
Missing terms

If a degree is missing from the polynomial (e.g., x³ + 2x − 1 has no x² term), you must insert a 0 placeholder in the coefficients: 1, 0, 2, −1. Forgetting this is the #1 synthetic division mistake.

Practice — Division
  1. (x² + 3x − 10) ÷ (x − 2) — try long division first
  2. (2x³ + x² − 5x + 2) ÷ (x + 1) — use synthetic division
  3. (x³ − 8) ÷ (x − 2) — watch for the missing terms!
03

Remainder Theorem & Factor Theorem

These two theorems connect division, remainders, and zeros into a single elegant idea. They're short to state but deeply useful.

Remainder Theorem

When a polynomial f(x) is divided by (x − c), the remainder equals f(c).

You don't need to do the full division to find the remainder — just evaluate the polynomial at x = c.

Worked Example
What is the remainder when f(x) = x³ − 4x + 1 is divided by (x − 3)?
By Remainder Theorem: remainder = f(3) f(3) = (3)³ − 4(3) + 1 = 27 − 12 + 1 = 16 Remainder = 16 // No long division needed at all.
Factor Theorem

(x − c) is a factor of f(x) if and only if f(c) = 0.

The remainder is zero exactly when c is a root of f(x). Factor Theorem is just the Remainder Theorem applied to the case where the remainder = 0.

Why this matters

Together these let you verify factors quickly. If you suspect (x − 2) is a factor of some polynomial, just check whether f(2) = 0. If it is, you know (x − 2) divides evenly with no remainder — so it's a factor.

Practice — Theorems
  1. Find the remainder when x⁴ − 2x³ + x − 5 is divided by (x − 1). Use the Remainder Theorem.
  2. Is (x + 2) a factor of x³ + 3x² − 4? Show your reasoning using the Factor Theorem.
  3. Find a value of k so that (x − 3) is a factor of x² + kx − 6.
04

Zeros of Polynomials & Multiplicity

A zero (also called a root) of a polynomial f(x) is a value c where f(c) = 0. By the Factor Theorem, every zero c gives a factor (x − c), and every factor gives a zero. They're the same thing from two angles.

Zeros ↔ Factors ↔ x-intercepts

These three things are always the same thing: a zero of f(x), a root of f(x) = 0, and an x-intercept of the graph of y = f(x). Make sure those three descriptions feel like the same object to you.

Multiplicity refers to how many times a particular root appears as a factor. A root c has multiplicity k if (x − c)ᵏ divides f(x) but (x − c)ᵏ⁺¹ does not.

Multiplicity and graph behavior f(x) = (x − 1)²(x + 3) Zeros: x = 1 (multiplicity 2), x = −3 (multiplicity 1) At x = −3 (odd multiplicity): graph CROSSES the x-axis At x = 1 (even multiplicity): graph TOUCHES and TURNS AROUND This is the key visual rule: odd → crosses, even → bounces.
Worked Example — Finding all zeros
Find all zeros of f(x) = x³ − 2x² − 5x + 6
Step 1: Try the Rational Root candidates: ±1, ±2, ±3, ±6 f(1) = 1 − 2 − 5 + 6 = 0 ✓ → (x − 1) is a factor Step 2: Divide out (x − 1) using synthetic division: 1 | 1 -2 -5 6 | 1 -1 -6 +------------------ 1 -1 -6 0 Quotient: x² − x − 6 Step 3: Factor the quotient: x² − x − 6 = (x − 3)(x + 2) All zeros: x = 1, x = 3, x = −2 (each multiplicity 1) // f(x) = (x − 1)(x − 3)(x + 2)
Rational Root Theorem

For a polynomial with integer coefficients, any rational zero must have the form p/q where p divides the constant term and q divides the leading coefficient. This gives you a finite list of candidates to test.

End behavior

The sign of the leading coefficient and whether the degree is even or odd completely determine what the graph does at far left and far right. Even degree → both ends go same direction. Odd degree → ends go opposite directions. Positive leading coefficient → right end goes up.

Practice — Zeros
  1. State the zeros and multiplicities: f(x) = (x + 1)³(x − 2)²
  2. At which zeros does the graph cross the x-axis? At which does it bounce?
  3. Find all zeros: f(x) = x³ + x² − 4x − 4
  4. A cubic polynomial has zeros at x = −1, 0, and 4. Write a possible equation for f(x).
05

The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra

Fundamental Theorem of Algebra

Every polynomial of degree n ≥ 1 with complex coefficients has exactly n zeros in the complex numbers, counted with multiplicity.

A degree-3 polynomial has exactly 3 zeros. A degree-5 polynomial has exactly 5 zeros. Always. (Some may be complex, some may be repeated.)

In Algebra 2 you mostly work with polynomials that have real coefficients. For those, a crucial corollary applies:

Complex Conjugate Root Theorem

If a polynomial has real coefficients and (a + bi) is a zero, then its conjugate (a − bi) is also a zero.

Complex roots always come in conjugate pairs when the coefficients are real. You can never have just one complex root.

Worked Example — Using FTA to find a polynomial
Write a degree-4 polynomial with real coefficients that has zeros: x = 3, x = −1, x = 2i
Since 2i is a zero and coefficients are real, −2i must also be a zero. So the four zeros are: 3, −1, 2i, −2i Factors: (x − 3)(x + 1)(x − 2i)(x + 2i) Combine complex pair first: (x − 2i)(x + 2i) = x² − (2i)² = x² + 4 Final answer: f(x) = (x − 3)(x + 1)(x² + 4) // Expand if needed; stays real because complex pair produced real factor
Summary — Counting zeros of a real polynomial Degree 1: always 1 real zero Degree 2: 2 real zeros, OR 1 repeated real zero, OR 2 complex conjugates Degree 3: 1 or 3 real zeros (complex zeros come in pairs, so can't have 2) Degree 4: 0, 2, or 4 real zeros Degree n: at least 1 real zero if n is odd
Practice — FTA
  1. A degree-5 polynomial with real coefficients has zeros 1, 2, 3, and (1 + i). How many zeros does it have in total? What is the fifth zero?
  2. Write a polynomial with real coefficients of least possible degree that has zeros x = 4 and x = 3i.
  3. Can a degree-4 polynomial with real coefficients have exactly 3 real zeros and 1 complex zero? Explain.
