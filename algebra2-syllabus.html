Purpose

A parent-led summer preview of the full Common Core Algebra 2 curriculum. The goal is conceptual fluency — understanding why the math works — not drill practice. Your son will arrive at class already knowing the terrain.

How to use this

Each week has 3 natural sessions. Start by asking "what do you already know?" before introducing anything. The physics parent notes are reminders of how each topic connects to what you already know deeply.

I
Domain
Polynomials, Rational & Radical Expressions
Weeks 1–2 · CC Standards: HSA-APR, HSA-REI
Week 01
Polynomial Operations & the Structure of Algebra
HSA-APR.A.1 · HSA-APR.B.2–3

Topics

  • Review: factoring quadratics (GCF, trinomials, difference of squares)
  • Polynomial long division & synthetic division
  • Remainder Theorem & Factor Theorem
  • Finding zeros of polynomials; multiplicity & graphical behavior
  • Fundamental Theorem of Algebra (statement)

Physics parent note

The Factor Theorem is the algebraic version of finding roots of characteristic equations — you did this in differential equations and normal modes. Multiplicity is why a critically damped oscillator's solution has a repeated root.

Week 02
Rational Exponents, Radicals & Complex Numbers
HSN-RN.A.1–2 · HSN-CN.A.1–3 · HSN-CN.C.7

Topics

  • Rational exponents ↔ radical equivalence (x^(p/q) = ∜xᵖ)
  • Simplifying radical expressions; rationalizing denominators
  • Imaginary unit i; complex numbers a + bi
  • Arithmetic of complex numbers (add, subtract, multiply, conjugates)
  • Solving quadratics with complex solutions via quadratic formula

Physics parent note

You know complex numbers well — impedance, wave functions, Euler's formula. Keep it simple here: your son just needs i² = −1 and the arithmetic rules. The geometric view (Argand plane) is a nice enrichment if he's curious.

Checkpoint 1 Can he factor any polynomial, divide it, and find all roots including complex ones?
II
Domain
Functions — Families, Transformations & Inverses
Weeks 3–4 · CC Standards: HSF-IF, HSF-BF
Week 03
Interpreting & Building Functions; Transformations
HSF-IF.A–C · HSF-BF.A.1 · HSF-BF.B.3

Topics

  • Domain, range, and function notation review
  • Even/odd functions; symmetry
  • Transformations: shifts, reflections, stretches (f(x) → af(bx−h)+k)
  • Piecewise functions and absolute value functions
  • Combining functions: (f+g), (f·g), f(g(x)) — composition

Physics parent note

Transformation fluency is essential for reading graphs fast. The a/b/h/k framework is the same one used to describe amplitude, frequency, phase, and offset in wave functions. This week builds visual intuition, not computation — draw everything.

Week 04
Inverse Functions; Square Root & Cube Root Functions
HSF-BF.B.4 · HSF-IF.C.7b

Topics

  • One-to-one functions; horizontal line test
  • Finding inverse functions algebraically and graphically
  • Domain restriction to create invertible functions
  • Graphing √x and ∛x and their transformations
  • Solving radical equations; checking for extraneous solutions

Physics parent note

Extraneous solutions are a key concept — they arise whenever you square both sides. Good moment to discuss why algebraic manipulations can introduce false solutions (you've seen this in physics when you square an equation to eliminate a radical). Always verify solutions.

Checkpoint 2 Can he graph any standard function family with transformations and find its inverse?
III
Domain
Exponential & Logarithmic Functions
Weeks 5–6 · CC Standards: HSF-IF.C, HSF-LE, HSF-BF
Week 05
Exponential Functions & e
HSF-IF.C.7e · HSF-LE.A.2–4 · HSF-BF.B.5

Topics

  • Exponential growth and decay: f(x) = abˣ
  • The number e and natural exponential function eˣ
  • Graphing exponential functions and transformations
  • Modeling: half-life, population growth, compound interest
  • Distinguishing linear vs. exponential growth from tables/graphs

Physics parent note

You know e deeply — RC circuits, radioactive decay, thermal equilibration. Help him see e not just as a number but as the base of natural growth. Compound interest is the cleanest first model; half-life gives the decay intuition without needing calculus.

Week 06
Logarithmic Functions & Equations
HSF-BF.B.5 · HSF-LE.A.4 · HSA-REI.D.11

Topics

  • Logarithm as the inverse of exponentiation: log_b(x) = y ↔ bʸ = x
  • Common log (log) and natural log (ln)
  • Properties of logarithms: product, quotient, power rules
  • Change of base formula
  • Solving exponential and logarithmic equations
  • Graphing logarithmic functions; domain restrictions

Physics parent note

Log properties are basically exponent rules in disguise. The hardest thing for students isn't the rules — it's trusting that log(ab) = log a + log b. You can show this is just the product rule for exponents. Decibels and Richter scale are great real-world motivators.

Checkpoint 3 Can he move fluidly between exponential and logarithmic form and solve equations in both?
IV
Domain
Trigonometric Functions
Week 7 · CC Standards: HSF-TF
Week 07
Trigonometry Extended to the Unit Circle
HSF-TF.A.1–4 · HSF-TF.B.5

Topics

  • Radian measure; converting degrees ↔ radians
  • Unit circle: defining sin, cos, tan for all angles
  • Key values: 0, π/6, π/4, π/3, π/2 and their multiples
  • Graphing y = sin x and y = cos x; amplitude, period, phase shift
  • Graphing y = tan x; asymptotes
  • Modeling periodic phenomena with sinusoidal functions

Physics parent note

This is the week where your physics background is most valuable. Amplitude, period, frequency, phase — you've used these constantly. The unit circle is just the geometric definition; radians are natural (arc length = rθ). He should understand sin/cos as projections, not just ratios.

Checkpoint 4 Can he sketch a sinusoidal function from its equation and write one from a graph?
V
Domain
Statistics & Probability
Week 8 · CC Standards: HSS-ID, HSS-IC, HSS-CP
Week 08
Data Analysis, Probability & Statistical Inference
HSS-ID.A.4 · HSS-IC.A–B · HSS-CP.A–B

Topics

  • Normal distribution: shape, mean, standard deviation, 68-95-99.7 rule
  • z-scores and percentiles
  • Sample vs. population; sampling variability
  • Interpreting margin of error and confidence intervals (conceptually)
  • Conditional probability; independence
  • Counting: permutations and combinations
  • Binomial probability (intro)

Physics parent note

Stats is often the weakest week in Algebra 2 classes — many teachers rush it. The normal distribution is worth lingering on: measurement error in lab is exactly this. The key conceptual leap is distinguishing a population parameter from a sample statistic. Real understanding here pays off in every science course.

The Three Big Threads

Functions as Objects

Algebra 2 is where students stop computing with functions and start reasoning about them. Transformation rules, inverses, composition — all of this is treating a function as a thing you can manipulate, not just evaluate.

Extending Number Systems

Each unit pushes the number system outward: real → complex, integers → rationals, rationals → irrationals. This theme runs from Week 1 through Week 3. Help him see it as one continuous story about what "number" means.

Modeling the Real World

Every function family has a natural home: polynomials for motion, exponentials for growth/decay, sinusoids for oscillation. The statistics unit asks: what does data tell us about an underlying model? This is the physicist's question.
